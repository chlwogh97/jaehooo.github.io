Bin.Go!

A desktop UI for council workers to manage garbage collection by autonomous collection robots in designated areas.
Companion Website for UI Demonstration - Link

"People ignore designs that ignore people."

Frank Chimero

C O N T E X T

Garbage disposal is something that everyone living in a household has to do - even at least once in their lifetime.

The current garbage collection system is ran by councils in charge of areas, where they allocate specific time and day where bins are collected from households.

Our aim of the project was to design a more effective and efficient garbage collection system that saves time, reduces noise pollution and clears up roads on busy times. It was mainly targeted to stakeholders including but not limited to:

  • Residents
  • Council executives
  • Council workers

R E S E A R C H  &  I D E A T I O N

Initial research for the chosen concepted involved gathering as much data as possible regarding the current system, which included user research methods such as: Online Survey, Focus Groups and Card Sorting.

From gathered data results, we created personas and storyboards who face multiple problems that are evident with the current garbage collection system. 

From these key insights from initial research and ideation, we realised that one of the most significant problems common from our findings was 'freedom of garbage collection through bin days'.

Making this issue the key element of our future researches, as a group we came up with 4 concepts:

  1. Autonomous garbage robots replacing garbage trucks
  2. Personal garbage robot bins for each household
  3. Autonomous underground chute
  4. Garbage Drone.

D E S I G N  &  I D E A T I O N

After evaluation of these 4 chosen concepts, we refined our ideas down to 2 - Autonomous bins, and Garbage collection robots as we thought that they are more realistic and practical.

We then moved onto discussing low-fidelity prototypes for these two ideas, where we used POP and InVision to create paper lo-fi prototypes.

We were also challenged with an obstacle where our tutor suggested that the second idea - personal robot bins lacked interactions that the users can make iwth the UI and the vehicle as features would only consist of users perssing some buttons and sending out the robot for it to carry out its only task - disposing bins and returning.

The team then took a different approach by thinking from the other end of the perspective - the council. We then brought in a new idea: a UI for the council to operate a robot rental service where residents can call/book in for a pickup at desired times, then the designated operator approves then sends the robot out. This feature would include booking management, other logistics related services and reporting incidents to other authorities for support.

To be more in depth, we decided to design UIs for both sides - the council and the residents. This approach helped us maximize the field of interaction.

We decided to use 3 user evaluation methods:

  1. Concurrent Think Aloud (CTA) - to understand particiapnts' thoughts as they attempt to work through issues they encounter while browing the prototype
  2. First Click Testing - allows us to evaluate the effectiveness of the linking structure of the website, such as navigation, to see if users know how to get around the site and complete their intended tasks
  3. System Usability Scale (SUS) - participants are asked to score 1- questions from 1 - 5 from strongly disagree to strongly agree. The questions mainly target the overall usability of the system and asks how users feel about using the UI

After gathered results from these methods, we decided to ideate for the second idea - council dashboard, as we agreed that it contained diverse interactions as they:

  • Have more possible interactions with the nature of the autonomous vehicle idea
  • Interesting design possibilities as we focus on the other side of the perspective - the council
  • More ways to make workplace interactive and less boring for workers

We then developed a mid-fidelity prototype using Figma based off results and suggestions, where we were able to collaborate online too, making the work process more convenient and efficient.

Just like the previous ideas, we used the same methods as it would give us direct comparisons with pervious insights and results and maintains consistency in design throughout the research.

Outcomes were beneficial, where participants agreed that there were improvements on the positive elements of the system, and decrease on the negatives. However, there as an increase on the number of participants wh thought that they would have to study the system before using it due to their lack of knowledge and/or the complexity of the system.

After developing the system user flow for the lo-fi council booking system, we went ahead to refine the system into a high-fidelity prototype, adding colours, changing layout and polishing other features while maintaining the same user flow that we created in the beginning.

After receiving more feedbacks through the use of same user evaluation methods, we've come to make our final prototype using Figma, then used Framer to code animations and add physical interactions.

R E F L E C T I O N

Bin.Go! is something that originally started from a very simple and genuine thought of "I can't be bothered taking the bins out every week!", and throughout the second semester of 2018, being taught the design processes and methods of creating user interfaces helped us endeavour this process further and bring it to life by making it into a working prototype.

Working with friends with similar ideas and different strengths in design helped all of us to understand the important element of design - it's about the people. A quote by Frank Chimero really expresses our key focus on our creation of this interface.
