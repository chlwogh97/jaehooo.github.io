1. BACKGROUND RESEARCH

During week 1, we learned about the design process, design concepts and ways to make user centred designs. I also managed to form a group of 3 to last for the semester, including myself, John Antonios and Han Yi Peng. We were told to make a UI for an autonomous vehicle for our major project for the semester.

During week 2 tutorial, we were taught how to draw a stakeholder map. As a group, we thought of three ideas for autonomous vehicles - wheelchair, ambulance and garbage truck since they all fit into the criteria of 'moving something from one point to another'.

We then moved onto researches and studies to figure out if there are existing studies regarding the mentioned vehicles above, and round that autonomous wheelchairs are already in production stage and there already are numerous research papers for the vehicle. Additionally, we agreed that for an autonomous ambulance, it would not be easy to gather data and interviews from paramedics and medical practitioners.

THANK GOD, not a lot of people thought of autonomous garbage trucks! There were a lot of information about the current system because it's run by the council, however not a lot of studies were present about autonomous garbage vehicles that are ready to be out for practical use.

So we went with that idea.

3. USER RESEARCH

In week 5 after our individual presentations, we collated all user research data from the first assessment, then made key insights. One of the most significant problems common in our findings was 'freedom of garbage collection through bin days'.

Making this issue the key element of our future researches, as a group we came up with 4 concepts:

  1. Autonomous garbage robots replacing garbage trucks
  2. Personal garbage robot bins for each household
  3. Autonomous underground chute
  4. Garbage Drone

As apart of our ideation process, we managed to create 3 storyboards for each ideas, making it 12.

In week 6 after evaluating on the 4 concepts, we have chosen our two strongest idea - Autonomous bins and garbage collection robots, because realistically speaking, the chute and the drone system had too many downsides for practical use. We then moved onto discussing low-fidelity prototypes for those two ideas, where we thought of 3D printing a phone and having a slide of sketched screens to use as a prototype. We also discussed various user evaluations such as a/b testing, click testing and think-aloud.

On the next week, we came up with an agreement that instead of paperproto and using 3D printed phone, it's more cost-efficient and easier to use a mobile app such as InVision and Marvel as they support more digital, still lo-fi prototyping while they allow users to physically interact with the sketched UI by tapping on the screen.

We were challenged with an obstacle when our tutor criticized our second idea - personal robot bins, as it lacked interactions that the users can make with the UI and the vehicle as the features would only consist of users pressing some buttons and sending out the robot for it to carry out its only task - dispose bins and return. So we had to bring in a new idea - a UI for the council to operate a robot rental service where residents can call/book in for a pickup at the desired time, then the operator designated for the sector approves then sends the robot out. The feature would include booking management, other logistics based services and reporting incidents to other authorities.

To be more in depth, we decided to design UI for both ends of the perspective - the council and the residents. This will maximize the field of interaction. For data gathering, we have managed to call up the council and receive some information and opinion from the customer service representative as they would be our target stakeholder for this design.

PreviousNext

On week 8, we were still on our way to finish the extended storyboards with 12 frames each and create lo-fi prototypes. We decided that it was ideal to grab 8 resident and operator participants for the first idea, and 8 operator participants for the second idea.

We also thought of useful user evaluation methods that could be utilised:

  • Concurrent Think Aloud (CTA) - to understand participants' thoughts as they attempt to work through issues they encounter
  • First Click Testing - which allows to evaluate the effectiveness of the linking structure of the website, such as nagivation, to see if users know how to get around the site and complete their intended tasks
  • System Usability Scale - participants are asked to score 10 questions with a score from 1 - 5 from Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree. The questions mainly target the overall usability of the system and and asks how users feel about using the UI.

After allocation of evaluation methods within group members, I have decided to do user evaluation through the use of SUS and went out to test both the resident and the management side to grab both quality and quantity of the feedback. Then I managed to gain 5 people, where 3 were from the resident view, and 2 with some form of management experience.

We made lo-fi sketched prototypes for both ideas 1 and 2, where idea 1 was designed for mobile devices used by residents, and idea 2 was designed as a web-based UI for council operators working on desks.

4. SYNTHESIS

On week 9 lecture, we were advised on the ideal structure of the visual report that is due the week after. Continuing with the assessment, we have come down in favour of the 2nd idea - council dashboard, as we agreed that more possible interactions as they:

  1. Have more possible interactions with the nature of the autonomous vehicle idea
  2. Interesting to design on the other side of the perspective
  3. More ways to make workplace interactive and less boring for workers

We then developed a mid-fi wireframe using Figma and InVision, then digitally annotated and created a user flow. And for more accurate and valid results, we refrained from interviewing participants who have already seen lo-fi sketches. This made it a bit difficult to find someone who has experience in management, therefore we decided to broaden our spectrum so instead of looking for people with experiences in customer/logistics/service management, we agreed that anyone with any sort of management would still be able to provide with insightful responses.

Developing the mid-fi fidelity, we've made several changes to the UI:

Adjust Bookings Landing
  • Prettified the table into tabs and a minimalistic design - amenable and more comfortable for users in a typical workplace with long hours
  • Removed the underlines anchor links under the Booking ID and included an EDIT button on the right hand side of each row
  • Removed the Delete Icon and added a checkbox with a darkened row when it is checked, and a delete selected button from iteration ONE at the bottom of the table
  • Added pagination in case not all bookings fit on the one instance of the table at the bottom right hand corner
  • Added tabs in the table: Current Bookings, Recently Added and To Be Done Today
Adjust Booking Individual
  • Added a back button to go back to the ‘Adjust Bookings Landing’.
  • Removed the Booking ID as it can’t be changed, it is a unique identifier (primary key) for each row of the table.
  • Removed the Name as it is not relevant for the booking.
  • Included different types of input forms: Dropdown for the service, Calendar for selecting a Day, Time for selecting a Time, and multiple checkboxes for choosing the types of bins.
  • Adding an undo changes button in case the user has mistakenly made a change or wants to revert back to the original booking.
Service Robots Landing
  • Removed the underlines anchor links under the Robot  ID and included a SERVICE button on the right hand side of each row.
  • Added pagination in case not all bookings fit on the one instance of the table at the bottom right hand corner.
  • Removed the ‘Last Service’ and ‘Dispatched’ columns.
  • Added a checkbox with a darkened row when it is checked, and a cancel selected button at the bottom of the table.
Contact Police
  • Removed address of the police station as it is not relevant for contacting them urgently.
  • Added a Call and Email button to contact the police directly.
Contact Residents
  • Toolbox for the interactive map: Click and drag, point and click, fill an area, zoom in and out.
  • Simplified buttons to contact residents.
Notifications
  • Label for the notifications, not just an icon on the main navigation.
  • Moved the action button to the right hand side for easier clicking.
Contact Technicians
  • Removed Phone Number and E-mail columns and combined them into one column with the respective emails and phone numbers for each techincian.
  • Added pagination in case not all bookings fit on the one instance of the table at the bottom right hand corner.
Infringements Landing
  • Removed the underlines anchor links under the Infringement ID and included a REVIEW button on the right hand side of each row.
  • Added pagination in case not all bookings fit on the one instance of the table at the bottom right hand corner.
Settings
  • Clickable user image on the navigation which goes to the user account settings.
  • Removal of the Colour Theme setting in the Settings menu.
  • Added a button for the link to work phone, to make a connection to the phone.
  • Added a button beneath confirm changes to reset the settings to default.

Other settings and design were included and excluded to maintain consistency throughout the user's navigation in the system.

Just like the first 2 ideas, we decided to implement the same methods as it gives us direct comparisons with previous insights and results and maintains consistency throughout the research.

Outcomes were beneficial, where participants agreed that there were improvements on the positive elements of the system, and decrease on the negatives. However, there was an increase on the number of participants who thought that they would have to study the system before using it due to their lack of knowledge and/or the complexity of the system. Some identified issues were:

  • Uncertainty around the buttons, where placements and sizes were not explicit enough to be salient
  • Lack of detail on the map feature - it would've been better if the map shows the robots' live location and status
  • Cannot check previous changes made to bookings and a robot's history of issues
  • Cannot check the in-depth progress of a booking or service other than seeing the robot's current location on a map.

We got to the final stage of high fidelity prototype production where as a group we went through the interaction. We realised that there were some iterations to be made to refine the overall aesthetics and functionalities of the website.

To make the UI more fluid, we decided to use prototyping tools to add animations and make the system more interactive. Since we were familiar with InVision as we created our lo-fi prototype with it, we went ahead to try InVision Studio. However the software lacked micro-interactions and some features that we wanted to use, and it felt more of a mid-fi prototyping instead of hi-fi.

We then tried to use Atomic, however the fact that it's a web-based program made the whole system buggy and slowed our process down. Then came the discovery of Framer. The software provided us with multiple features that we needed to use for our prototyping.

One of the downsides was the fact that our entire system is based with a side navigation bar, however there are no methods of having a fixed side navbar, but only the header and the footer. This created a conflict of either giving up adding majority of the animations for the prototype to be seamless, or having animations, but lacking the natural feel that websites have.

Using Framer, we managed to add some extra features such as pop up boxes where necessary to improve the overall interaction between the interface and the users. Additionally, we thought that it would be more visually appealing to apply 3D maps in pages that require maps and it was agreed that some users that need extra help with finding directions and locations would benefit from using this feature.

To finalise our assessment, our group member John has got his friend as the narrator to help with the walkthrough video of the high fidelity prototype. Assessment 4 was submitted on the 25th of October 2018.
